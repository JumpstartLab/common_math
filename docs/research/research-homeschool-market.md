# Homeschool Market Research: Math Curriculum and Supplementary Tools

## Executive Summary

The homeschool market represents a compelling — and underserved — channel for a math learning app with the features described in the Paper Math Companion concept. The population is large (3.4M+ students), growing at nearly three times the pre-pandemic rate, and concentrated in grade bands 4-8 where parent math confidence erodes. The market spends meaningfully on curriculum ($300-1,500/year per child), is shifting toward digital/subscription models, and has specific pain points — parent math anxiety, unfamiliar strategies, limited tutoring access — that the app directly addresses. EngageNY/Eureka Math has a meaningful homeschool user base, especially among secular families, and the free content layer is genuinely novel in this market.

---

## 1. Homeschool Market Size and Growth

### Current Population

- **3.408 million K-12 homeschool students** in the United States during the 2024-2025 school year (NHERI estimate), representing approximately 6.3% of the school-age population.
- A separate JHU Institute for Education Policy tracking study (using state-by-state enrollment data) estimated 4.6 million homeschool students as of 2023-2024, suggesting NHERI's methodology may undercount. The true number likely falls in the 3.5–4.5M range.
- For comparison: homeschooling is roughly **double Catholic school enrollment** (1.684M) and approaching public charter school levels (3.784M).

### Post-COVID Growth

- Pre-pandemic baseline: ~2.5 million students (spring 2019).
- Current growth rate: **4.9% annually** — nearly three times the pre-pandemic rate of ~2%.
- 80% of reporting states showed year-over-year increases in 2024-2025.
- 36% of reporting states set new all-time enrollment records in 2024-2025, **exceeding even pandemic peaks**.
- Notable state growth rates (2024-2025): South Carolina +21.5%, Vermont +17%, Ohio +15%, New Hampshire +14.5%.
- The NCES (National Center for Education Statistics) confirmed in a September 2024 press release that a higher percentage of K-12 students are receiving academic instruction at home than at any prior measurement point.

### Why It Matters for This Product

The growth is not reverting. Families that switched to homeschooling during COVID and stayed are now multi-year practitioners who have invested in curriculum and tools. The market has matured past the emergency-homeschooling phase into a stable, self-selecting community of committed families — exactly the kind of users who pay for subscriptions and leave reviews.

## 2. Total Addressable Market

### Market Size Estimates

Market research estimates for the global homeschool curriculum market (2024):
- Business Research Insights: **$2.48 billion**
- Verified Market Research / other sources: **$3.24–3.5 billion**
- North America share: approximately **$1.46 billion** (dominant region)

Projected growth:
- CAGR of 6.6%–8.7% through 2033-2035
- Market projected to reach **$5–6.76 billion** by 2033

The online homeschool programs sub-market specifically was valued at **$4.2 billion globally in 2024** and is projected to grow at 12.4% CAGR, reaching $12.1 billion by 2033 — reflecting the shift toward digital delivery.

### Per-Family Spending

Annual curriculum/materials spending per child:
- **HSLDA estimate: $300–600/year** (curriculum only)
- Broader estimates including enrichment, extracurriculars: **$700–1,800/year per child**
- Full cost range: $500–3,500/year depending on approach and curriculum choices

These numbers are highly variable because a family using only free resources (Khan Academy, EngageNY PDFs, library books) can spend near zero, while a family buying complete packaged curriculum from Math-U-See plus supplements can spend $600+ on math alone.

**Key insight:** The $8-12/month pricing for the app's paid tier sits at $96-144/year — comfortably within the discretionary supplement budget for most homeschool families, and below what many already spend on a single math subscription (Teaching Textbooks family plan: $199/year; CTCMath family: $198/year).

## 3. How Homeschool Families Choose Math Curriculum

### Most Popular Math Curricula

Homeschool math curriculum choices cluster around a handful of well-established programs. In rough order of community visibility:

**1. Saxon Math**
- Long-standing, proven spiral approach; uses drills to reinforce skills
- Particularly strong among structured/classical homeschoolers
- Strong reputation for rigor; can feel dry or repetitive to some students

**2. Singapore Math**
- Problem-solving and critical thinking focus; concrete-to-abstract progression
- Strong reputation for depth; popular among academically ambitious families
- Several editions (Primary Mathematics, Math in Focus, Dimensions Math)

**3. Math-U-See**
- Multisensory, manipulative-based (plastic blocks)
- **500,000+ families** use the program — one of the largest installed bases in homeschool math
- Mastery (not spiral) approach; each level focuses on one topic until mastered
- Particularly strong with struggling learners and ADHD
- Pricing: Instruction Pack $70-90/level (reusable) + Student Pack ~$30/level + one-time block kit ~$80

**4. Teaching Textbooks**
- Digital/app-based; highly self-directed
- Automated grading, step-by-step explanations for every problem
- **Most frequently cited "easy button" for homeschool math** — minimal parent involvement required
- Pricing: $43-67/level individual, or **$199/year family plan** (unlimited children)
- Consistent 4+ star reviews; parents specifically praise it when they're not confident in math

**5. RightStart Mathematics**
- Abacus-based; strong visual/spatial approach
- Higher price point; intensive parent involvement

**6. Math With Confidence (Kate Snow)**
- Newer entrant (K-6); designed explicitly for homeschool parents with math anxiety
- Growing community following

**7. Art of Problem Solving (AoPS)**
- Advanced; targets mathematically talented students
- Separate market segment from the mainstream

**8. Khan Academy**
- Free; used as primary or supplementary curriculum
- 168.7 million registered users globally; significant homeschool penetration
- Parent registration grew 20x since 2020 — proxy for homeschool adoption
- Often used to fill gaps or as free alternative to paid programs

**9. CTCMath**
- Online video-based; family pricing $29.95/month or $198/year
- Targeted at busy families wanting low parental involvement
- Particularly popular with struggling learners

**10. Mr. D Math**
- Online, accredited (WASC)
- Middle and high school focus; live teacher-led courses
- Targets families who want structured accountability

### How Families Discover and Evaluate Curriculum

Discovery happens through a layered ecosystem:

**Trusted review authorities:**
- **Cathy Duffy Reviews** (cathyduffyreviews.com) — the gold standard since 1984; her 102 Top Picks book is widely referenced. Getting a positive Cathy Duffy review is a meaningful market signal.
- **Well-Trained Mind community forums** (forums.welltrainedmind.com) — high-engagement discussion forums; curriculum threads get hundreds of replies
- **TheHomeSchoolMom.com** — aggregated community reviews
- **Homeschool.com** — top picks lists and product reviews

**Peer communities:**
- Facebook groups are the primary real-time discovery channel. Notable groups include dedicated secular homeschool groups, Charlotte Mason groups, classical education groups, and general support groups. The top Facebook homeschool groups have tens to hundreds of thousands of members. Curriculum recommendation threads routinely get 50-200+ comments.
- Co-ops: Families in co-ops share curriculum information, review materials together, and pool resources for group pricing. Co-op leaders have outsized influence on curriculum choices for their group.
- **Homeschool Buyers Club** (formerly Buyers Co-op) — 380,000+ member families; organizes group purchasing discounts; maintains convention lists

**Conventions:**
- **Great Homeschool Conventions** — largest US homeschool convention series; 5 regional locations; families attend from 38+ states. Vendor hall described as the "largest Exhibit Hall in the USA." 2026 vendor booths already sold out at multiple locations (Southeast, Missouri, Ohio, Texas). Conventions are where families handle physical curriculum in person, watch demos, and make purchasing decisions.
- Christianbook.com and Rainbow Resource attend conventions and are major curriculum retailers in their own right.
- Attending a convention before committing to an annual curriculum is a common pattern.

**Blogs and podcasts:**
- Niche homeschool bloggers with loyal followings (Treehouse Schoolhouse, Rock Your Homeschool, The Organized Homeschooler, etc.)
- Julie Bogart (Brave Writer), Sally Clarkson, and other philosophy-oriented voices shape approach before families select specific curriculum

**Word of mouth** is cited most consistently as the primary driver — not advertising.

### Typical Purchasing Model

The market is bifurcated:
- **Traditional curricula** (Saxon, Singapore, Math-U-See, RightStart): One-time purchase per level. Books, manipulatives, and teacher's manuals bought outright. Reusable across siblings. Parents buy used copies at co-op curriculum fairs or on Facebook Marketplace.
- **Digital/online curricula** (Teaching Textbooks, CTCMath, Khan Academy, Time4Learning): Annual subscription or monthly subscription. Teaching Textbooks' shift from CD-ROMs to subscription was initially controversial but is now accepted.
- **Supplementary tools**: Monthly subscriptions normalized at $5-15/month price points. Families stack multiple subscriptions (a core curriculum plus a drill app, plus a reference tool).

The trend is clearly toward subscription-based digital tools as supplements, while families often keep a physical curriculum as the "spine."

## 4. EngageNY / Eureka Math and Homeschoolers

### Usage Pattern

Homeschool families do use EngageNY/Eureka Math, primarily as a **free secular math resource**, but the usage pattern differs significantly from schools:

- Families access free PDFs from archive sites (EngageNY.org shut down July 2022; materials remain available via NYSED and community mirrors like EMBARC.online)
- Great Minds sells printed Homework Helper books and parent-facing materials for families using Eureka Math at home
- Khan Academy maintains dedicated course tracks aligned to Eureka Math/EngageNY (6th grade, 7th grade, etc.) — indicating meaningful homeschool crossover
- Outschool lists Eureka Math as a resource homeschool families can use

### Why Homeschoolers Use It

- It's **free** — significant advantage over $300-600/year packaged curricula
- **Secular** — no religious content, important to the growing secular homeschool segment (37% of homeschoolers cite nonreligious motivations per 2023 Pew data)
- **Rigorous** — Eureka Math's reputation for depth appeals to academically ambitious homeschool families
- **Common Core aligned** — families in states that still use CCSS can use it without gaps

### Why Homeschoolers Avoid It

- **Dense and teacher-directed** — the curriculum assumes a credentialed teacher with planning time; the Fluency, Application Problem, Concept Development, Student Debrief lesson structure can feel overwhelming for a parent
- **No teacher support** — no parent-facing "how to teach this" resources in the free materials
- **Strategy vocabulary unfamiliar to parents** — the same problem the Parent Decoder feature addresses
- **EngageNY.org is dead** — finding materials requires knowing where to look
- One review describes Eureka Math as "Singapore Math's colorless cousin" — rigorous but not engaging

**Key insight:** EngageNY is used by a cost-conscious, secular, academically motivated homeschool segment — but it's underserved because the materials assume school-context support that families don't have. This is exactly the gap the app could fill: EngageNY content + parent support layer + AI tutor.

## 5. Homeschool Math Pain Points

### Pain Point 1: Parent Math Confidence

This is the defining challenge in homeschool math, especially grades 4-8:
- Studies show 20-25% of children experience math anxiety — and parent math anxiety is directly transmitted to children
- Research explicitly links highly math-anxious parents with math-anxious children
- Homeschool alumni frequently report that parent math knowledge gaps prevented them from getting help when stuck
- Teaching Textbooks' primary selling proposition is explicitly "you don't have to be good at math to use this"

The problem intensifies around grade 4-5 when multi-digit multiplication, fractions, and long division arrive with unfamiliar strategy names (area models, partial quotients, standard algorithm vs. non-standard strategies). Parents who learned "the old way" face material their children are being taught differently.

### Pain Point 2: Unfamiliar Strategy Vocabulary

Modern math curricula teach multiple strategies and name them explicitly (area model, partial products, number bonds, bar models, open number lines). Parents who went to school before these methods were standard don't recognize them and can't help. This is precisely the Parent Decoder use case.

### Pain Point 3: Knowing When to Move On vs. When to Reteach

Mastery vs. spiral decisions. Parents don't have the assessment training to know if a gap is fundamental or temporary. This leads to either moving too fast (Swiss cheese understanding) or getting stuck and losing momentum.

### Pain Point 4: Limited Scaffolded Practice Options

When a child doesn't understand something, parents often don't know how to decompose the concept and approach it differently. They search for "more worksheets" but find either the same problem type (no help) or completely different material (too different).

### Pain Point 5: Curriculum Isolation

Homeschoolers don't have a classroom or colleagues. When stuck on a teaching problem, they post to Facebook groups and wait. There's no "ask the math coach" option. AI support has real appeal here.

### Pain Point 6: Tutoring Cost

Private math tutoring averages $70-120/hour (National Tutoring Association data). For families where math is genuinely difficult, this is a significant expense. A 2024 Pew Research survey found **60% of parents report their child has required extra help in math over the past year**. AI tutoring at $8-12/month is an order of magnitude cheaper.

### Demand for "Structured Curriculum + AI Support When Stuck"

The demand signal is clear:
- 68% of parents are open to AI learning tools if they improve outcomes and reduce costs (2024 Pew Research)
- Khanmigo testimonials from homeschool families describe it as transformative for families that need deeper concept explanations
- Synthesis Tutor (AI math tutor for K-5, ~$99-120/year) has 4.7 stars from 8,000+ App Store reviews — strong validation that homeschool families will pay for AI math support
- The specific gap: existing AI tools (Khanmigo, Synthesis) don't do camera input, don't decode unfamiliar strategies for parents, and don't connect to the student's specific textbook

## 6. Existing Homeschool-Focused Math Products

### Teaching Textbooks
- **Model:** Self-paced digital subscription; app-based (iOS, Android, Mac, Windows)
- **Pricing:** $43-67/level (individual) or $199.08/year (family plan, unlimited children)
- **Approach:** Spiral; automated grading; video explanations for every problem; minimal parent involvement
- **Reputation:** Strongest in the "math-anxious parent" and "independent learner" segments; some criticism of being too easy (not rigorous enough for advanced students)
- **Competitive position:** Dominates the "I need math to teach itself" niche; not a Socratic tutor, doesn't support paper-based work

### Math-U-See
- **Model:** Mastery-based; manipulative-heavy; one-time purchase per level
- **Pricing:** ~$70-90 instruction pack + ~$30 student pack/level + ~$80 block kit (one-time)
- **Approach:** Video lessons by founder Steve Demme; physical blocks for visualization
- **Reputation:** 500,000+ families; excellent for struggling students and hands-on learners; popular in ADHD communities
- **Competitive position:** High physical engagement; no AI; no camera; not screen-based

### Khan Academy / Khanmigo
- **Khan Academy:** Free; massive homeschool penetration; used as primary or supplementary
- **Khanmigo:** AI tutor; free for families in the US (as of recent announcements); Socratic style
- **Gap:** No camera input; no paper-work loop; no parent strategy decoder; no textbook indexing; video-based instruction model

### Synthesis Tutor
- **Model:** AI math tutor; conversational; K-5 focus
- **Pricing:** ~$99-120/year (annual plan); ~$45/month
- **Reputation:** 4.7 stars, 8,000+ App Store reviews; strong parent reviews; described as more engaging than Khan Academy's video format
- **Gap:** K-5 only; no camera input; no paper-work loop; no connection to specific curriculum/textbook

### CTCMath
- **Model:** Online video lessons + adaptive practice; family plan $198/year
- **Approach:** Short tutorial videos + interactive questions; detailed progress tracking
- **Reputation:** Popular with struggling learners; "low parent involvement" positioning
- **Gap:** No AI interaction; no paper support; no camera

### Mr. D Math
- **Model:** Accredited online courses; live instruction option; middle and high school
- **Pricing:** Not disclosed publicly; course-based
- **Reputation:** Fully accredited (WASC); positions for transcript/diploma needs
- **Gap:** Aimed at older students; live-instruction model; no AI

### DreamBox Learning
- **Model:** Adaptive AI math; $12.95/month; K-8
- **Reputation:** Established adaptive engine; primarily school-market but available to consumers
- **Gap:** Screen-based game format; no paper; no camera; no parent decoder

### Smartick
- **Pricing:** $39.99/month (with sibling discounts)
- **Approach:** Daily 15-minute AI-driven sessions; ages 4-14; mastery focus
- **Gap:** Daily practice tool; no paper component; no parent strategy support

### MagicSchool AI
- Teacher-facing (lesson planning, rubrics); not a student or parent tool

### Photomath / Mathway / Gauth
- Camera → answer pipeline; no Socratic component; no parent support; no curriculum connection; widely seen as homework-cheating tools
- Strong brand recognition could actually help ("like Photomath, but it actually teaches you")

## 8. Regulatory Considerations

### State Variation in Oversight

Homeschool regulation exists on a spectrum:

**No oversight states** (e.g., Texas, Alaska, Idaho):
- No notification required
- No testing requirements
- No curriculum approval
- Standards alignment is irrelevant for legal compliance; families choose it because they want quality, not because they're required to demonstrate it

**Low-moderate oversight states** (majority):
- Notification to school district required
- Some require subject area coverage (math, science, English, social studies) but not specific curricula
- Standards alignment helps families feel confident they're covering required subjects, but specific state standard alignment is not mandated

**High oversight states** (New York, Pennsylvania, Massachusetts, Vermont, Rhode Island):
- Annual testing or portfolio assessment required
- Curriculum documentation may be required
- New York is particularly strict: detailed educational plan submitted annually, quarterly assessments, year-end evaluation

### Standards Alignment Value to Homeschoolers

For the **high-oversight states**, standards alignment documentation has genuine legal utility — it helps families demonstrate compliance.

For the **majority of states**, standards alignment has marketing value rather than legal necessity:
- It signals curriculum quality and rigor to parents who are uncertain about whether they're "doing enough"
- It provides a familiar quality benchmark (especially for parents who came from a school environment)
- It reduces anxiety about "falling behind" relative to grade-level expectations

**The free EngageNY content modernized with current state standards alignment is particularly compelling** because:
1. EngageNY is already widely used by cost-conscious, secular homeschool families
2. The materials haven't been updated since 2022; states have revised standards since then
3. Parents currently using EngageNY PDFs have no easy way to know if a lesson maps to current standards
4. Providing this mapping gives families a defensible answer to "are we meeting state standards?" — valuable even in low-oversight states where the question is about quality confidence, not legal compliance
5. In high-oversight states (NY especially), it's directly useful for annual plans

### Notable: EngageNY's Origin in New York

EngageNY was created by the New York State Education Department. New York is a high-oversight state. Families in New York using EngageNY already have strong alignment to NY standards — the value-add would be for families in other states who adopted EngageNY but now need to map it to their state's current standards.

## 9. Strategic Implications for Paper Math Companion

### Why This Market Is Attractive

1. **Size with real spending capacity:** 3.4-4.5M students; $300-600/year curriculum budgets; subscription models are normalized
2. **Underserved by existing AI tools:** Khanmigo has no camera or paper-work loop; Synthesis is K-5 only; Teaching Textbooks is not AI-interactive
3. **Direct match to pain points:** Parent math anxiety, unfamiliar strategy vocabulary, scaffolded practice, "I need a tutor but can't afford one" — all are acute in this market
4. **Secular growth segment:** 37% nonreligious motivation homeschoolers represent a fast-growing segment actively seeking quality secular math resources; EngageNY appeals directly to them
5. **Distribution channels are well-mapped:** Review sites, conventions, Facebook groups, bloggers — all reachable without large ad budgets
6. **Non-compete alignment:** The homeschool B2C channel completely sidesteps the non-solicitation restriction on K-12 school/district customers

### Positioning Recommendation

Position primarily as a **parent support and student homework companion** rather than a replacement curriculum. The message: "Works with whatever curriculum you're using — take a photo of any problem." This avoids competing with Teaching Textbooks or Math-U-See and instead complements them.

The free EngageNY content layer serves as acquisition. For families already using EngageNY (or willing to try it because it's free), the app becomes the support infrastructure that makes EngageNY viable as a standalone homeschool curriculum — which is currently its biggest weakness.

### Key Distribution Actions

1. **Submit to Cathy Duffy Reviews** — highest single-action reach in the market
2. **Exhibit at one regional Great Homeschool Convention** — test demo effectiveness; collect testimonials
3. **Target secular homeschool Facebook groups** with organic posts and parent testimonials
4. **Approach Kate Snow (Math With Confidence) and similar math-anxiety-focused voices** for potential partnerships or reviews
5. **Optimize for "EngageNY homeschool" searches** — families actively searching for EngageNY support are pre-qualified

### Pricing Fit

$8-12/month ($96-144/year) is well-positioned:
- Below Teaching Textbooks family plan ($199/year)
- Below CTCMath family plan ($198/year)
- Below Synthesis Tutor ($99-120/year for K-5 only)
- Well below private tutoring ($70-120/hour)
- Within the discretionary supplement budget of most homeschool families
- Monthly option reduces friction for trial

A family plan option (2-3 children for a modest upcharge) would align with how homeschool families think about per-child curriculum costs and would increase LTV for the most committed homeschool buyers.
