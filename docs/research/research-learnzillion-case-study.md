# LearnZillion Case Study: Building a Business on Freely Available Curriculum

**Research date:** March 2026
**Purpose:** Precedent analysis for building a product on top of openly licensed curriculum content (EngageNY/Eureka Math)

---

## Company History

### Founding

LearnZillion was founded in 2011 by Eric Westendorf and Alix Guerrier, both based in Washington, D.C. Westendorf had been principal of E.L. Haynes Public Charter School in D.C., where he began creating instructional math videos with his staff. The origin story is instructive: Westendorf realized he didn't fully understand how to divide whole numbers by fractions and was impressed by an elegant explanation from a sixth-grade math teacher — one he wanted to share with other teachers across the country. He initially cobbled together a site using YouTube, Google Docs, and Weebly.

Westendorf studied at Stanford (MBA) and later partnered with classmate Alix Guerrier to scale the solution. Guerrier eventually left to become CEO of GlobalGiving; Westendorf remained CEO through the acquisition.

### Original Vision: Teacher-Created Video Lessons

The initial product was a library of supplemental instructional videos, created by practicing classroom teachers explaining K-12 math and English concepts. The distinguishing premise: teachers teaching teachers, not publishers packaging curriculum.

In summer 2012, the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation funded a "Dream Team" — 120 teachers who convened in Atlanta to collaboratively produce approximately 2,000 instructional videos. This both seeded the content library and validated a model: the collaborative process itself turned out to be valuable professional development for the participating teachers. The Gates Foundation also provided a separate $965,000 grant to support the platform.

### Business Model Evolution

LearnZillion went through three distinct business model phases:

**Phase 1 (2011–2017): Free supplemental content + PD revenue**
Content was free to teachers and students. Revenue came from charging school districts for professional development services associated with implementing the materials. This is essentially the "give away the razor, sell the blades" model applied to curriculum.

**Phase 2 (2018–2019): Pivot to district curriculum sales**
In 2018, LearnZillion announced a major strategic shift: it would compete directly with major textbook publishers (Pearson, McGraw-Hill, Houghton Mifflin) by selling full-course curriculum to school districts as an enterprise product. The offering bundled openly licensed curriculum from Illustrative Mathematics (middle-school math) and EL Education (K-5 ELA) with:
- Professional development
- Print and digital delivery
- Scope-and-sequence documentation
- Pacing guides
- Assessment tools
- Teacher implementation support

The underlying curriculum content remained openly licensed; LearnZillion was selling the packaging, delivery, and support infrastructure.

**Phase 3 (2019–acquisition): Streamlined core curriculum company**
In May 2019, LearnZillion laid off approximately 20 employees (leaving 34 staff), cutting sales, customer success, and engineering. The company was slimming down to reach breakeven, narrowing focus to core curriculum sales. CEO Westendorf said at that point the company might not need to raise more capital.

### B2B Focus

LearnZillion was firmly B2B. Their customers were school districts, not individual teachers or parents. Free access for individual teachers was maintained as a top-of-funnel strategy, but revenue came from institutional district contracts. Teachers with up to one class could access content free; schools and districts with 100+ students paid an annual fee starting at $5,000.

## Curriculum Relationships

### EngageNY / Eureka Math

LearnZillion does not appear to have built a primary product line around EngageNY or Eureka Math content. Khan Academy is the most prominent organization that created aligned support materials for Eureka Math/EngageNY. LearnZillion's curriculum portfolio centered on Illustrative Mathematics and EL Education content rather than Great Minds / Eureka Math material.

This is a meaningful distinction for our use case: **the EngageNY/Eureka Math space was not heavily occupied by LearnZillion**, which suggests there is less precedent to follow from them directly on that specific curriculum — but also potentially less competition in that niche.

### Illustrative Mathematics (IM)

This was LearnZillion's primary math curriculum partner and the centerpiece of their commercial curriculum offering. Key dynamics:

- **Formal partnership with revenue share:** IM and LearnZillion had an explicit arrangement where IM received a share of LearnZillion's revenue, which IM used to continue improving its curriculum. This model allowed a nonprofit curriculum developer to sustain itself financially while a commercial company handled distribution and support.
- **IM Certified status:** LearnZillion was the first IM-Certified curriculum provider. The certification meant LearnZillion could only make enhancements to the original content if IM approved them as meeting the curriculum's goals. This quality gate protected the curriculum's integrity while also giving LearnZillion a defensible differentiator from uncertified competitors.
- **Named product:** LearnZillion Illustrative Mathematics became a branded product line that earned strong EdReports ratings.
- Post-acquisition, it is now called **Imagine IM** under Imagine Learning.

### EL Education

LearnZillion announced a formal partnership with EL Education in 2019 to deliver EL Education's top-rated K-5 Language Arts curriculum through a combination of software and print, available for the 2019-2020 school year.

### Louisiana Guidebooks (State OER)

A notable non-IM example: LearnZillion partnered with the Louisiana Department of Education to distribute "Guidebooks 2.0" — ELA curriculum created by the state itself. Louisiana's Guidebooks were used in 80% of districts statewide. LearnZillion provided the digital platform and print production; the content was state-authored. This became the "LearnZillion Guidebooks" product.

### Great Minds / Eureka Math — No Direct Relationship

No evidence of a partnership or formal relationship between LearnZillion and Great Minds (which publishes Eureka Math, the commercial version of EngageNY). Great Minds pursued their own distribution and had complex views on commercial OER use. The company was not a customer or partner of LearnZillion.

## Licensing of OER Content

### CC-BY License Foundation

The core Illustrative Mathematics and EL Education curricula were released under Creative Commons Attribution (CC-BY) licenses. CC-BY permits:
- Freely sharing, copying, and distributing the material
- Adapting and building upon the material
- Commercial use

The only requirement is attribution to the original creator. This is the most permissive Creative Commons license, making it legally straightforward for commercial companies to build on the content.

### The Attribution Conflict

In 2019, Open Up Resources — which held the original license on the IM middle-school curriculum — accused a "group of publishers" (including LearnZillion) of failing to provide adequate attribution. Their position: materials should display "Download for free at openupresources.org" on every page. LearnZillion's position: they were following CC-BY guidelines and providing attribution on every digital lesson, digital page, and print product.

The dispute was eventually resolved. However, it exposed a genuine tension in the OER commercial market: **the same curriculum was available from multiple competing commercial providers** (LearnZillion, Kendall Hunt, McGraw-Hill), all selling similar products built on the same free underlying content. Open Up Resources argued that commercial providers were using the OER brand to attract customers without making clear that the core content was free.

LearnZillion CEO Westendorf's defense was principled and commercially important: "For OER to be successful, there have to be business models that work and allow for an [Illustrative Mathematics] curriculum to become a curriculum that competes with a Pearson, a McGraw-Hill, a Houghton Mifflin." He framed commercial OER distributors as necessary infrastructure for OER to achieve scale.

### Scale Claims

At peak, LearnZillion claimed:
- Used by more than 1 in 3 teachers nationwide
- Coverage of more than 80% of U.S. school districts

These figures likely reflect free individual teacher accounts rather than paying district customers.

### Revenue

By 2021 (post-acquisition, under Weld North), reported revenue was approximately $1.9M — a modest figure suggesting the company never achieved large-scale commercial traction despite its user reach. The low revenue relative to stated user scale underscores the challenge of monetizing a freemium model in K-12.

## The Acquisition

### Acquirer: Weld North Education (not Imagine Learning directly)

A critical clarification: LearnZillion was **not** acquired by Imagine Learning. It was acquired by **Weld North Education** on January 8, 2020. Weld North is the parent holding company that also owns Edgenuity and Imagine Learning. The deal terms were not disclosed.

Weld North described itself as "the largest pure-play digital education company." Its existing portfolio focused on digital intervention, supplemental, and remedial products — not core curriculum. LearnZillion was Weld North's first K-12 core curriculum acquisition.

### Strategic Rationale

Weld North CEO Jonathan Grayer stated that his long-term plan was to "build an education company that could change the way teachers teach kids" centered on digital curriculum with data capture and adaptive feedback capabilities. The existing portfolio couldn't meet core curriculum needs, and LearnZillion filled that gap.

Specific strategic value cited:
1. **Core curriculum distribution platform** — LearnZillion would serve as a platform for other curricula in Weld North's portfolio (Imagine Learning, Compass Learning)
2. **Student reach** — the acquisition added approximately 400,000 students to Weld North's footprint, bringing the total to roughly 7.4 million students across 20,000 schools
3. **District relationships** — LearnZillion's existing district sales relationships were valuable for cross-selling

### Post-Acquisition Trajectory

Following the acquisition, LearnZillion was integrated into Imagine Learning (Weld North's consumer-facing brand). The product was rebranded as **Imagine Learning Classroom**, with ilclassroom.com as the platform URL. The curriculum lines were renamed:
- LearnZillion Illustrative Mathematics → **Imagine IM**
- LearnZillion EL Education → **Imagine Learning EL Education**
- LearnZillion Guidebooks → **Imagine Learning Guidebooks**

The ilclassroom.com platform remains accessible as of early 2025, though Imagine Learning has increasingly restricted the free tier. In summer 2021, significant changes were made to what free users could access. The trajectory has been toward requiring district subscriptions for meaningful access.

As of 2024-2025, Imagine Learning expanded the portfolio with:
- **Imagine IM** (grades K-8): the IM v.360 edition with accessibility and usability enhancements
- **Traverse**: a new digital-first social studies curriculum for grades 6-12
- High school IM courses expected for fall 2025

## Lessons for Our Use Case

### What Worked

**1. CC-BY licensing makes the legal structure clean**
Building on CC-BY content is legally unambiguous. Attribution is required; commercial use is explicitly permitted. LearnZillion operated openly on this basis for years, and the courts/marketplace resolved in their favor during the Open Up Resources dispute.

**2. Adding value through infrastructure, not content**
LearnZillion's value add was never about having better math content than Illustrative Mathematics — it was about making that content usable at the district level. Print production, pacing guides, scope-and-sequence docs, assessment tools, professional development, and a software delivery platform are all things school districts need but that nonprofit curriculum developers typically don't provide.

**3. Certification as a quality gate and competitive differentiator**
Being the first "IM Certified" provider gave LearnZillion both credibility (districts trusted the quality) and a competitive barrier (competitors couldn't claim the same certification). For EngageNY/Eureka Math content, Great Minds controls Eureka Math certification, but EngageNY itself is uncertified public domain — which is different.

**4. State partnerships as a distribution strategy**
The Louisiana Guidebooks partnership shows that state education agencies are themselves a channel. States that create their own OER curriculum need delivery infrastructure; LearnZillion provided it. This is a potentially underutilized angle for EngageNY content, since New York State discontinued the EngageNY.org site in 2022 and the content is now distributed through archives.

**5. Revenue sharing with nonprofit content creators**
The IM revenue share arrangement created alignment: IM had incentive to promote LearnZillion's product because it funded IM's operations. This is a model worth examining for partnerships with EngageNY-adjacent organizations.

### Pitfalls and Challenges

**1. Low revenue despite high user numbers**
LearnZillion's reported $1.9M revenue against claims of reaching 80% of districts suggests a profound freemium conversion problem. Free individual teacher access did not translate to district-level sales at meaningful scale. The company was still not profitable after 8+ years and $30M+ raised.

**2. Commoditization — multiple providers of the same content**
Once the IM curriculum became popular, multiple commercial providers entered the market (LearnZillion, Kendall Hunt, McGraw-Hill). Districts could evaluate competing products built on identical underlying content. This is the core risk of the "OER wrapper" business model: competitors can do exactly what you do at lower cost, and there is no proprietary content advantage.

**3. Attribution disputes create legal and reputational risk**
The Open Up Resources cease-and-desist episode (even though ultimately resolved) shows that relationships with OER licensors can become contentious as commercial stakes grow. OER community members often have philosophical objections to commercial exploitation of free content, and those objections can manifest as public criticism even when legal footing is solid.

**4. Nonprofit curriculum developers eventually go direct**
Illustrative Mathematics has increasingly become a direct publisher, not just a content licensor. The long-term risk for any OER wrapper business is that the content creator decides to compete directly. Great Minds pursued exactly this path with Eureka Math, taking what was EngageNY and building a direct commercial curriculum with its own support services.

**5. Freemium creates a free rider problem**
Making curriculum freely available to individual teachers while charging districts creates a permanent tension: teachers can download everything for free and self-implement, undermining the district sales pitch. The value proposition has to be "easier/better for districts at scale" rather than just "access to the content."

**6. The exit required venture capital and ultimately a strategic buyer**
LearnZillion raised ~$30M and was ultimately acquired by a large strategic buyer. The company never reached independent profitability in its original form. The acquirer's value was primarily strategic (market position, district relationships, product line) rather than financial. Founders and investors likely had modest returns given the revenue figures.

### Differentiating from "Just a Wrapper"

LearnZillion's differentiators were:
- Certification status (IM Certified — quality gate controlled by IM)
- Professional development integration
- Teacher-facing implementation guides
- Print production and fulfillment
- Software platform (ilclassroom.com) with structured lesson delivery, student tracking
- District-level sales and support infrastructure
- State partnerships (Louisiana)
- First-mover advantage in their curriculum lines

The companies that ultimately commoditized them (McGraw-Hill especially) could replicate most of these except the certification status and the existing district relationships.

### Competitive Moat Assessment

LearnZillion's moat was relatively thin:
- **Not content** — the content was free and available to anyone
- **Not technology** — the platform was not technically differentiated
- **Partially relationships** — district relationships have switching costs, but are not insurmountable
- **Partially certification** — IM Certified status was valuable but controlled by a third party (IM), which could revoke or extend to competitors

The acquisition at a modest revenue figure suggests the market agreed the moat was narrow. What ultimately had value was the assembled customer relationships and the established product brands, not an enduring competitive position.

## Relevance to EngageNY/Eureka Math Use Case

### Key Differences from the LearnZillion Model

1. **EngageNY is in the public domain (not just CC-BY)** — New York State government content is public domain. This is even more permissive than CC-BY; no attribution is required. There is no Great Minds or Open Up Resources equivalent holding a license that could generate attribution disputes.

2. **Great Minds went commercial with Eureka Math** — Great Minds took the EngageNY content and built Eureka Math, a commercial product with its own support ecosystem. This means the EngageNY curriculum already has a major commercial incumbent (Eureka Math), but the underlying public domain content remains freely available. This is analogous to LearnZillion's situation except that the "nonprofit content creator" in this case became a direct competitor rather than a partner.

3. **No certification structure exists for EngageNY** — Unlike IM Certified providers, there is no credentialing body for EngageNY-aligned products (except EdReports ratings, which any publisher can seek).

4. **EngageNY.org was shut down in 2022** — The state discontinued the official site, creating a distribution gap. Content is now on archive sites. This is actually an opportunity: there is unmet demand for a well-maintained, searchable, accessible version of the content.

### Applicable Lessons

- The value-add model (infrastructure + support around free content) is validated by LearnZillion's existence and acquisition
- B2B district sales is the right target market; free individual teacher access can be top-of-funnel
- Certification or quality signaling (e.g., EdReports ratings) is worth pursuing early as a differentiator
- Revenue sharing with curriculum-adjacent organizations (state DEs, curriculum coaches) could create distribution partnerships
- The moat should be built on distribution relationships and switching costs (teacher workflow integration), not content control
- Keep the free tier genuinely useful — LearnZillion's broad teacher reach was ultimately what made it worth acquiring, even at modest revenue
