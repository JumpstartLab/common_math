# Conversation Guide: Eric Westendorf (LearnZillion Founder)

## Context

Eric founded LearnZillion in 2011, built it on openly licensed curriculum content (primarily Illustrative Mathematics), raised $29-32M, reached 80% of US districts, and sold to Weld North Education (Imagine Learning parent) in January 2020. Revenue at acquisition was reportedly ~$1.9M despite massive reach — a cautionary datapoint about OER-to-revenue conversion in K-12.

Jeff knows Eric personally. This conversation is to learn from his experience before making similar bets.

## Opening

"I recently left Great Minds and I'm exploring some ideas in the math edtech space. You've been through the exact journey I'm considering — building on open curriculum content, figuring out the business model, navigating the K-12 market. I'd love to pick your brain on what worked, what didn't, and what you'd do differently with today's tools."

## Questions

### The OER Business Model

1. "LearnZillion had incredible reach — 80% of US districts — but converting that to revenue was clearly the hard part. What was the core challenge? Was it the free-to-paid transition, the sales cycle length, or something structural about how schools buy?"

2. "If you were starting over today, with AI capabilities that didn't exist in 2011, would you still go B2B/enterprise, or would you consider going direct to parents and students?"

3. "What do you wish you'd known in year one about building a business on top of someone else's openly licensed content?"

### The IM Partnership

4. "How did the Illustrative Mathematics partnership come together? Was being 'IM Certified' a meaningful differentiator in sales, or mostly a constraint?"

5. "Would you recommend seeking a formal partnership with a curriculum publisher, or staying independent and building on the OER directly?"

### Great Minds / Eureka Math

6. "Did LearnZillion ever work with EngageNY/Eureka Math content, or was it always IM? Was that a deliberate choice?"

7. "Do you have any read on how Great Minds views third-party tools built around their original OER content? Are they aggressive about it or do they mostly ignore the ecosystem?"

### The Acquisition & Defensibility

8. "When Weld North acquired LearnZillion, what did they actually value — the technology platform, the content, the district relationships, or the team?"

9. "Looking back, what would you have done differently to build a more defensible position?"

### My Specific Situation

10. "I'm considering a B2C parent subscription model — an iOS app where parents and students use their phone camera to get AI-powered math tutoring support while working on paper assignments. $8-12/month. Does direct-to-parent feel viable to you in math ed, or does the money ultimately have to come from institutions?"

11. "I'm also thinking about a separate effort to modernize the frozen EngageNY content — parse it into structured data, realign to current state standards, publish it free under CC BY-NC-SA as a top-of-funnel for the paid app. Does that strategy make sense to you, or is free-content-as-funnel a trap you've seen before?"

12. "Did you ever seriously consider the homeschool market? 3.4-4.5 million students, growing fast, direct-to-parent purchasing, no district gatekeepers. Real opportunity or distraction?"

### The Current Landscape

13. "You've had some distance from it now. What did the edtech market learn — or fail to learn — from the post-COVID correction?"

14. "Does AI fundamentally change the OER play, or is it the same market dynamics with better tools?"

## Listen For

- **Specific failure modes** — not just "it was hard" but exactly where conversion broke down
- **Whether he'd go B2C** — his answer here is probably the single most valuable signal
- **His read on Great Minds** — insider perspective on how they'd view a competitor using their OER
- **Regrets about defensibility** — what moat would he build if doing it again?
- **Reaction to the camera + paper + AI concept** — does it resonate as genuinely different, or does it sound like another app?
- **Homeschool market reaction** — "yes, we should have" vs. "we looked at it and it's too fragmented"
